Evaluation and scoring

Policies are ranked by their mean per-scenario normalized score. This page explains the metric behind the standings.

Per-scenario normalized score

Each scenario is normalized against a fixed baseline set, then averaged with equal weight over all scenarios.

$$ \text{score} = \frac{r - r_{\text{worst}}}{r_{\text{best}} - r_{\text{worst}}} $$
r: the policy's episodic return on a scenario. r_best, r_worst: best and worst returns of the fixed baselines on that same scenario.

The overall score is the average normalized score over all training scenarios: 10 public buildings × 6 weather scenarios = 60 development environments. A score of 1.0 means the policy matches the best baseline everywhere; above 1.0 means it beats every baseline.

Final evaluation runs on the held-out set: 10 buildings × 6 weather scenarios × 3 rollouts = 180 scored trajectories. The code supports unlimited scenario generation, so participants can sample arbitrarily many additional buildings during development, while the held-out evaluation buildings stay completely unseen.

Each score is reported with a bootstrapped 95% confidence interval that quantifies how much the mean would move under stochastic occupancy and weather. Two policies are treated as significantly different only when their intervals are disjoint, which guards against reading a ranking gap that is within noise.

Grouping by weather or building is deliberately avoided: any grouping imposes an arbitrary weighting and hides within-group variance. On a common 0 to 1 scale, a flat equal-weight mean is the cleanest choice, so each building and weather pair counts exactly once. A raw mean of returns is avoided because scale would dominate it: winter scenarios swing by tens of reward units while temperate ones swing by a few.
